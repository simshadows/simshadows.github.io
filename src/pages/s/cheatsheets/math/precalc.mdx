    <CSTitle>Partial Fraction Decomposition</CSTitle>

    A *proper rational function* can be rewritten as a sum of *partial fractions*.

    For each irreducible factor in the denominator, the partial fractions are as follows:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        \begin{array}{ccccc}
            \substack{\text{\underline{irreducible factor in denominator}}} &&&&
                \substack{\text{\underline{partial fractions}}}
                \\[1ex]
            \displaystyle {(\memphR{ax + b})}^k &
                & \Longrightarrow & \quad &
                %\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^k{\frac{A_n}{\memphR{(ax + b)}^n}}
                \displaystyle \frac{A_1}{\memphR{ax + b}}
                + \frac{A_2}{\parens{\memphR{ax + b}}^2}
                + \dots
                + \frac{A_k}{\parens{\memphR{ax + b}}^k}
                \\[3ex]
            \displaystyle {(\memphR{ax^2 + bx + c})}^k &
                & \Longrightarrow & \quad &
                %\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^k{\frac{A_n x + B_n}{\memphR{(ax^2 + bx + c)}^n}}
                \displaystyle \frac{A_1 x + B_1}{\memphR{ax^2 + bx + c}}
                + \frac{A_2 x + B_2}{\parens{\memphR{ax^2 + bx + c}}^2}
                + \dots
                + \frac{A_k x + B_k}{\parens{\memphR{ax^2 + bx + c}}^k}
        \end{array}
    " />

    For *improper rational functions*, you must first convert it to a *proper rational function*.

    *Hint: You can use complex numbers to further reduce some factors. Example: <Eq code="\parens{1 + x^2} = \parens{1 + i x} \parens{1 - i x}" />*
</CSFrame>

    <CSTitle>Hyperbolic Function Exponential Definitions</CSTitle>

    <AlignLatex code="
        \sinh{x}
            &= \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}
            = \frac{e^{2x} - 1}{2 e^x}
            = \frac{1 - e^{-2x}}{2 e^{-x}} \\
            %\quad \forall x \in \mathbb{R} \\
        \cosh{x}
            &= \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}
            = \frac{e^{2x} + 1}{2 e^x}
            = \frac{1 + e^{-2x}}{2 e^{-x}} \\
            %\quad \forall x \in \mathbb{R} \\
        \tanh{x}
            &= \frac{\sinh{x}}{\cosh{x}}
            = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}
            = \frac{e^{2x} - 1}{e^{2x} + 1}
    " />
</CSFrame>

    <CSTitle>Dot Product / Scalar Product</CSTitle>

    In <Eq code="\Reals^n" />:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + \cdots + a_n b_n = \sum_{k=0}^n{a_k b_k}
    " />

    In <Eq code="\Reals^2" /> or <Eq code="\Reals^3" />:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \abs{\mathbf{a}} \abs{\mathbf{b}} \cos{\theta}
        ,\qquad \theta \in [0, \pi]
    " />

    Useful geometric properties:
    - <Eq code="\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a} = \abs{a}^2" />, hence <Eq code="\abs{\mathbf{a}} = \sqrt{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{a}}" />.
    - Vectors <Eq code="\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n" /> are orthogonal if <Eq code="\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0" />.
</CSFrame>

<CSFrame>
    <CSTitle>Cross Product / Vector Product</CSTitle>

    The *cross product* is only defined in <Eq code="\mathbb{R}^3" />.
    <DisplayLatex code="
        \begin{pmatrix}
            a_1 \\
            a_2 \\
            a_3
        \end{pmatrix}
        \times
        \begin{pmatrix}
            b_1 \\
            b_2 \\
            b_3
        \end{pmatrix}
        =
        \begin{pmatrix}
            a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2 \\
            a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3 \\
            a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1
        \end{pmatrix}
    " />

    Calculation using determinants:

    <GatherLatex code="
            \begin{pmatrix}
                a_1 \\
                a_2 \\
                a_3
            \end{pmatrix}
            \times
            \begin{pmatrix}
                b_1 \\
                b_2 \\
                b_3
            \end{pmatrix}
            =
            \begin{vmatrix}
                \mathbf{e}_1 & \mathbf{e}_2 & \mathbf{e}_3 \\
                a_1          & a_2          & a_3          \\
                b_1          & b_2          & b_3
            \end{vmatrix}
        \\
            =
            \mathbf{e}_1
            \begin{vmatrix}
                \memphR{a_2} & \memphB{a_3} \\
                \memphB{b_2} & \memphR{b_3}
            \end{vmatrix}
            -
            \mathbf{e}_2
            \begin{vmatrix}
                \memphR{a_1} & \memphB{a_3} \\
                \memphB{b_1} & \memphR{b_3}
            \end{vmatrix}
            +
            \mathbf{e}_3
            \begin{vmatrix}
                \memphR{a_1} & \memphB{a_2} \\
                \memphB{b_1} & \memphR{b_2}
            \end{vmatrix}
        \\
            \begin{aligned}
                = \mathbf{e}_1 &\parens{\memphR{a_2 b_3} - \memphB{a_3 b_2}} \\
                &- \mathbf{e}_2 \parens{\memphR{a_1 b_3} - \memphB{a_3 b_1}} \\
                &+ \mathbf{e}_3 \parens{\memphR{a_1 b_2} - \memphB{a_2 b_1}}
            \end{aligned}
    " />

    Useful geometric properties:
    - Vector <Eq code="\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}" /> is orthogonal to <Eq code="\mathbf{a}" /> and <Eq code="\mathbf{b}" />.
    - <Eq code="\abs{\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}} = \abs{\mathbf{a}} \abs{\mathbf{b}} \sin{\theta} = \text{area of a parallelogram}" />
</CSFrame>

<CSFrame>
    <CSTitle>Cramer's Rule</CSTitle>

    Consider the following linear system with <Eq code="n \times n" /> invertible matrix <Eq code="A" />:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}
        ,\qquad A \in M_{nn},\ \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n,\ \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n
    " />

    The system has a unique solution:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        x_k = \frac{\det{\parens{B_k}}}{\det{\parens{A}}}
        ,\qquad \forall k = 1, \dots, n,
    " />

    where <Eq code="B_k" /> is the matrix obtained from <Eq code="A" /> by replacing the <Eq code="\Nth{k}{th}" /> column with the vector <Eq code="\mathbf{b}" />.

    <CSTitle>Cramer's Rule, <Eq code="2 \times 2" /> Matrix</CSTitle>

    <DisplayLatex code="
        \begin{dcases}
            \xmemphB{a_{11}} x + \xmemphB{a_{12}} y = \xmemphR{b_1} \\
            \xmemphB{a_{21}} x + \xmemphB{a_{22}} y = \xmemphR{b_2}
        \end{dcases}
        \quad \Rightarrow \quad
            \xmemphB{
                \begin{bmatrix}
                    a_{11} & a_{12} \\
                    a_{21} & a_{22}
                \end{bmatrix}
            }
            \begin{bmatrix}
                x \\
                y
            \end{bmatrix}
            =
            \xmemphR{
                \begin{bmatrix}
                    b_1 \\
                    b_2
                \end{bmatrix}
            }
    " />

    <DisplayLatex code="
        x
            = \frac{
                \xmemphB{
                    \begin{vmatrix}
                        \xmemphR{b_1} & a_{12} \\
                        \xmemphR{b_2} & a_{22}
                    \end{vmatrix}
                }
            }{
                \xmemphB{
                    \begin{vmatrix}
                        a_{11} & a_{12} \\
                        a_{21} & a_{22}
                    \end{vmatrix}
                }
            }
            ,\qquad
        y
            = \frac{
                \xmemphB{
                    \begin{vmatrix}
                        a_{11} & \xmemphR{b_1} \\
                        a_{21} & \xmemphR{b_2}
                    \end{vmatrix}
                }
            }{
                \xmemphB{
                    \begin{vmatrix}
                        a_{11} & a_{12} \\
                        a_{21} & a_{22}
                    \end{vmatrix}
                }
            }
    " />

    <CSTitle>Cramer's Rule, <Eq code="3 \times 3" /> Matrix</CSTitle>

    A function <Eq code="f" /> is a rational function if it can be written in the form:

    <DisplayLatex code="

    " />

    <DisplayLatex code="

    *The fundamental theorem of calculus is so useful that you should just know it intuitively.*

    <CSTitle>First Fundamental Theorem of Calculus</CSTitle>

    Let <Eq code="f" /> be a continuous real-valued function defined on <Eq code="[a, b]" />, and let <Eq code="F" /> be defined by:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        F : [a, b] \to \myReals
            \qquad F(x) = \int_a^x{f(t) \,\diff{t}} .
    " />

    <Eq code="F" /> is continuous on <Eq code="[a, b]" />, differentiable on <Eq code="(a, b)" />, and has a derivative <Eq code="F'" /> given by:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        F'(x) = f(x)
            \qquad \forall x \in (a, b) .
    " />

    <CSTitle>Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus</CSTitle>

    Let <Eq code="F" /> be a real-valued function on <Eq code="[a, b]" />, and <Eq code="F" /> be an antiderivative of <Eq code="f" /> on <Eq code="[a, b]" />. Then:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        \int_a^b{f(t) \,\diff{t}} = F(b) - F(a)
    " />

<CSFrame>
    <CSTitle>Completing the Square</CSTitle>

    Suppose we have a quadratic function:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        f(x) = a x^2 + b x + c
    " />

    We want to express the same quadratic in the form:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        f(x) = A(x + B)^2 + C
    " />

    We can expand this form, then equate coefficients:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        f(x) = A x^2 + 2ABx + A B^2 + C
    " />
    <DisplayLatex code="
        A = a \qquad 2AB = b \qquad A B^2 + C = c
    " />

    Solving for <Eq code="A" />, <Eq code="B" />, and <Eq code="C" />, we get our function:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        f(x) = a \parens{x + \brackets{\frac{b}{2a}}}^2 + \brackets{c - \frac{b^2}{4a}}
    " />

    Thus, we have our general form for completing the square.

    However, it can be a bit unwieldy. If we instead assume we have a monic (i.e. <Eq code="a=1" />), then:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        f(x)
        = \parens{x + \xmemphR{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}}}^2
        + c
        - \xmemphP{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}^2}
    " />

    This form is easier for me to memorize since you just remember where <Eq code="\memphR{\frac{b}{2}}" /> and <span style="color: var(--color--mypurple);">its square</span> appears.

    Though, if one forgets <span style="color: var(--color--mypurple);">the square</span>, you can guess the form of the outside by expanding <Eq code="\parens{x + \memphR{\frac{b}{2}}}^2" />:

    <AlignLatex code="
        f(x)
            &= \parens{x + \xmemphR{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}}}^2 + c + \text{something} \\
            &= \parens{x^2 + 2 \xmemphR{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}} + \xmemphP{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}^2}} + c + \text{something}
    " />

    This *something* must be such that the constant doesn't change:

    <DisplayLatex code="
        f(x)
        = \parens{x^2 + 2 \xmemphR{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}} + \xmemphP{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}^2}} + c - \xmemphP{\brackets{\frac{b}{2}}^2}
    " />
</CSFrame>
